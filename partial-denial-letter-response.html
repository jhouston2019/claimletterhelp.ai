What is a Partial Denial or Underpayment?

A partial denial (also called underpayment) occurs when your insurance carrier pays less than you believe is owed under your policy. The insurer may cite depreciation, actual cash value (ACV) calculations, coverage limits, or valuation disagreements.

Partial denials are common in property claims where insurers and policyholders disagree on the value of damaged property or the cost of repairs.

Common Partial Denial Scenarios

Depreciation Deductions

Insurer pays actual cash value (ACV) instead of replacement cost, deducting depreciation from claim amount.

Valuation Disputes

Insurer's estimate or appraisal is lower than your estimate or independent appraisal.

Partial Coverage Denials

Insurer pays for some items but denies others, claiming certain damages are not covered.

Coverage Limit Issues

Insurer claims payment is limited by policy sub-limits or coverage caps.

⚠️ CRITICAL WARNING

Do NOT cash the payment check if you dispute the amount. Cashing the check may constitute acceptance of the settlement and waive your right to dispute the valuation. Review settlement language carefully before depositing any payment.

How This System Prepares Underpayment Responses

Procedural Valuation Dispute Notification

System prepares a structured, procedural response with the following fixed sections:

  • Header – Your contact information, insurer address, claim number, payment date
  • Acknowledgment – Brief statement acknowledging receipt of payment (2 lines max)
  • Valuation Dispute – State factual discrepancies only (3 lines max, no arguments)
  • Supporting Valuation – Optional reference to independent estimates (3 lines max)
  • Request for Review – Request reconsideration of valuation (2 lines max)
  • Payment Status – State whether check has been cashed (1 line)
  • Contact Information – Your phone and email (3 lines max)
  • Closing – Standard signature block

Total response length: Maximum 25 lines. No arguments. No bad faith claims. No threats.

What Underpayment Responses Will NOT Include

  • No bad faith accusations – System does not accuse insurer of bad faith or unfair practices
  • No legal arguments – System does not make legal claims or interpret policy language
  • No emotional or adversarial language – System maintains neutral, factual tone
  • No threats of litigation – System does not threaten legal action
  • No persuasive framing – System does not attempt to persuade or negotiate
  • No narrative explanations – System states factual discrepancies only

Critical: Payment Check Considerations

⚠️ Before Cashing the Check

Review the following BEFORE cashing or depositing any payment check:

  • Check for "full and final settlement" language – This may waive your right to additional payment
  • Review accompanying letter – Look for language indicating payment is final or in full satisfaction
  • Check endorsement language – Back of check may contain restrictive endorsement language
  • Consider state law – Some states allow you to cash check with written reservation of rights
  • Consult attorney if unsure – For significant amounts, get legal advice before cashing

If You've Already Cashed the Check

If you've already cashed the check, you may have waived your right to dispute the amount. However, in some cases you may still have options. Consult an attorney immediately to understand your rights under your state's law.

Independent Valuations and Estimates

Obtaining Independent Valuations

To support your valuation dispute, you may obtain:

  • Independent contractor estimates – Get 2-3 estimates from licensed contractors
  • Professional appraisals – Hire licensed appraiser for property valuation
  • Public adjuster – Consider hiring public adjuster to represent your interests
  • Comparable sales data – For total loss claims, provide comparable vehicle or property sales
  • Replacement cost quotes – Obtain actual replacement cost quotes from vendors

Appraisal Clause

Many insurance policies contain an "appraisal clause" allowing either party to demand binding appraisal of the loss value. If you and your insurer cannot agree on valuation, you may invoke this clause. Each party selects an appraiser, and the two appraisers select an umpire. The appraisers' decision (or umpire's decision if appraisers disagree) is binding.

When System Will Refuse to Prepare Underpayment Response

⛔ Hard Stop Conditions

System will refuse to prepare underpayment responses if payment letter contains:

  • Fraud allegations – Any mention of fraud or misrepresentation
  • Bad faith counterclaims – Insurer alleging you acted in bad faith
  • Attorney involvement – Payment letter mentions attorneys or legal action
  • Subrogation disputes – Complex subrogation or recovery issues
  • Coverage disputes – Partial payment combined with coverage denial or reservation of rights
  • High-value commercial claims – Commercial claims over $25,000 require professional representation

These scenarios require attorney representation. System will refuse output and recommend professional consultation.
